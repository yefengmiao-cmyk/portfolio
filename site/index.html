From Millions to Billions: Amazon Last Mile Delivery Transformation

Unrecognized Users→Recognized Users
1 · Learning
2 · Applying
3 · Onboarding

Problem

Scale, scale, and scale! According to forecast, Amazon logistics will experience exponential growth in volume of packages. Hence, Amazon Logistics needs a low-maintenance and efficient process to recruit, onboard, and manage thousands of new Delivery Service Partners (DSP).

Target User

Entrepreneurs who are interested in partnering with Amazon Logistics to open a delivery company.

Solution

My design solution enabled an end-to-end self-service acquisition and onboarding experience to enable scale.

Team

1 Lead product designer (me) 1 Researcher 1 Design technologist 4 PMs 1 TPM 1 EM 4 Engineers

Timeline

Program Discovery and Definition 2017 Active Design Time Jan - Mar 2018 (Program launched in June 2018) Post Launch Research Sept - Nov 2018

Phase 1: Discover

Market Research

The goal was to identify the top target audience and evaluate their interest in DSP 2.0 program.

Workshops

The goal was to understand the pain points of the current owners of the DSP 1.0 program.

Pilot Program

Evaluate the new DSP 2.0 concept with a few pilot entrepreneurs.

Note: The existing Delivery Service Partner Program is referred to as DSP 1.0. The recreated program is identified as DSP 2.0.

Phase 2: Define

An end-to-end self-serve acquisition and onboarding experience is critial to program success. The scope of the project covers three steps: Recruiting (Learning) Vetting (applying) Onboarding

Phase 3: Design

Step 1: Recruiting (Learning) We took a “brochure” approach. It’s not an “Operational Manual” which provides every single detail about the program. It’s also not a poster which has limited information. With the “Brochure” approach, our goal was to provide the right amount of information for entrepreneurs to make an informed a decision without feeling overwhelmed. (Note: This marketing site follows a different design system than the next two steps. We decided to align the marketing site with other Amazon Logistics public facing visual style.)
Step 2: Vetting (Applying) We took a “Guided Wizard” approach. The application process is broken down to smaller steps to allow applicants to focus on one thing at a time. Different levels of inline help is provided so that applicants has all the information needed to complete the application on their own.
Step 3: Onboarding We started with testing two design concepts: “Learning by doing” and “Guided Wizard.” “Learn by doing” is like “Typeform” experience. Instructions and guidance is embedded in the core task itself as you complete the task. “Guided Wizard” is like the application flow. There is an onboarding flow, separated from the main portal experience, it’s similar to Airbnb and TurboTax.

Neither tested well. The challenge is a lack of visibility into dependencies between the tasks and holistic view of the onboarding progress. Each task is isolated. We continued to explore and decided to go with the “Checklist” concept. In this concept, we list out all the tasks on the onboarding homepage, with each task linked to the section in the Delivery Service Partner App.

The program was launched in June 2018. It was extremely and unexpectedly popular.

2.Not enough time for approved DSP owners to set up all the logistics (e.g. recruiting, insurance, and etc.) before starting to deliver.

Flexport Unified Sub-Ledger (USL)

Problem

Flexport’s system architecture didn’t ensure 100% capture and coverage of all the revenue and costs associated with every shipment transaction service. This leads to:

Business consequences

Bottleneck for scale · high operation cost · financial leakage · compliance risks.

User challenges

Account Managers lack the time, quality data, and visibility into shipment financials to proactively manage net revenue at a shipment level. The Accounting team runs a tedious, time-consuming process to collect, file, and report — and results are still inaccurate.

Solution

After validating that off-the-shelf solutions don’t meet our unique needs for tracking shipment-related financials, we built a homegrown USL system to capture all revenue and costs across every shipment transaction service.

My role & timeline

Design leader coaching a team of 3 designers across invoicing, billing, and USL domains to define the right process and products for an efficient month-end close.

May 2022 – Jan 2023

To understand the current state, we had to understand how costs and revenue get captured throughout the shipment lifecycle. We ran 10+ stakeholder interviews and a three-day workshop to map the current state and identify where the process breaks.

Phase 2: Define

Next, we defined all the use cases, the scope, and the phases.

Phase 3: Design

Results

We aimed to launch phase 1 of USL in early 2023. We planned to measure: time reduced for Account Managers to capture costs and revenue of a shipment; time reduced for the Accounting team’s month-end close; and dollars reduced on financial leakage.

Building a High-Performing Design Team

4 Focus Areas to Build a High-Performing Design Team

I led a two-day team offsite to understand the challenges we needed to address to build a high-performing design team. As a result, we identified four areas to focus on.

Culture

We are problem solvers.

Design is more than just UI. Great design relies on challenging assumptions, understanding business goals, and being able to think critically.

We operate Lean.

It’s okay not to get it right straight out of the gate. We experiment, learn, and iterate quickly based on data and qualitative evidence.

We are stronger together.

We seek to understand each other, assume positive intent, and leverage our strengths and experiences to deliver the right idea to the right audience together.

We respect boundaries.

We clearly define our roles and responsibilities so that we hold ourselves and our cross-functional partners accountable.

We make space for fun.

To be creative, we create time and place to play together, and we celebrate the wins along the way.

Process

Defining a cross-functional design process
01 Discover
What is the problem? Is it the right problem to solve?
Key elements: problem statement, context, impact value, deciders.
02 Define
How might we solve the problem? What’s the scope?
Key elements: ideas and boundaries, resource estimation, key milestones, roles & responsibilities, success criteria.
03 Design + Validate
How can we push ahead together?
Key elements: refined ideas, design iterations, testing and validation plan.
04 Build
How do we build and ship it?
Key elements: design specs, implementation plan.
05 Sustain
Did our solution solve the problem? Any gaps to close?
Key elements: success metrics, new ideas.
